Effect of Biodiversity Loss

The silent unraveling of life's web — and what it means for humanity's survival on this planet.

20267 min readThe Digital Green Journal

Biodiversity — the extraordinary variety of life on Earth, from microorganisms to whales, from mosses to rainforests — is the foundation upon which all ecosystems function. It is the invisible infrastructure of life. Yet today, species are disappearing at a rate 100 to 1,000 times higher than the natural background extinction rate, driven almost entirely by human activity.

What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity encompasses three levels: genetic diversity (the variety of genes within a species), species diversity (the number and variety of species in an area), and ecosystem diversity (the range of ecosystems in a region). All three are interconnected, and the loss of any one component can trigger cascading effects throughout the others.

Healthy biodiversity provides essential ecosystem services that humans depend on: clean air and water, fertile soils, pollination of crops, climate regulation, disease control, and raw materials for medicine and food. When biodiversity is lost, these services weaken — and humanity pays the price.

Causes of Biodiversity Loss

Habitat Destruction

The conversion of natural habitats — forests, wetlands, grasslands, and coral reefs — for agriculture, urban development, and resource extraction is the leading driver of biodiversity loss. When a habitat is destroyed, the species that depend on it lose their homes, food sources, and breeding grounds.

Climate Change

Rising temperatures, shifting seasons, ocean acidification, and sea-level rise are altering the conditions species need to survive. Many cannot adapt quickly enough to these rapid changes. Coral reefs — among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth — are particularly threatened by warming oceans and acidification.

Pollution

Chemical pollutants — pesticides, fertilizer runoff, plastic waste, heavy metals — contaminate soil, water, and air, poisoning organisms throughout the food chain. Plastic pollution has now been found in the stomachs of seabirds, fish, and even deep-sea creatures.

Invasive Species and Overexploitation

Non-native species introduced to new environments can outcompete, prey upon, or introduce disease to native species. Overfishing, poaching, and unsustainable harvesting deplete populations faster than they can recover.

Effects of Biodiversity Loss

🌾

Food Security

75% of global food crops depend on animal pollination. Loss of pollinators directly threatens agricultural productivity worldwide.

💊

Medicine & Health

Over 50% of modern medicines are derived from natural compounds. Many undiscovered species may hold cures for diseases we haven't yet beaten.

🌊

Ecosystem Collapse

Keystone species play outsized roles in ecosystems. The loss of one can trigger cascading collapses throughout entire systems.

🌦️

Climate Regulation

Forests and wetlands absorb CO₂ and regulate rainfall. As these ecosystems are damaged, their ability to buffer climate change decreases.

🐠

Ocean Health

Coral reefs support 25% of all marine life. With reefs bleaching at record rates, millions of fish species and coastal communities are at risk.

🦠

Disease Spillover

Biodiversity loss increases the risk of zoonotic diseases — illnesses that jump from animals to humans — as natural buffers break down.

"We are the first generation to feel the effect of climate change and the last generation that can do something about it — and the same is true of biodiversity."
David Attenborough, Naturalist and Broadcaster

Biodiversity in the Philippines

The Philippines is one of the world's 18 megadiverse countries and a recognized global biodiversity hotspot. It hosts approximately 52,177 described species, of which about 67% are found nowhere else on Earth (endemic). This includes the Philippine Eagle — the world's largest eagle — the Tamaraw, the Irrawaddy dolphin, and hundreds of endemic plant species.

However, deforestation, coral reef degradation, and illegal wildlife trade have pushed many Philippine species to the brink. The country has lost over 90% of its original forest cover. Protecting Philippine biodiversity is not just an environmental issue — it is a cultural and economic imperative.

Philippine Biodiversity Highlights

  • Over 52,000 described species, with ~67% endemic to the Philippines
  • Home to the Philippine Eagle, Tamaraw, and Irrawaddy dolphin
  • Second most biodiverse country per unit area in the world
  • Tubbataha Reef — a UNESCO World Heritage Site and richest marine ecosystem in the Pacific
  • The Coral Triangle — including Philippine waters — contains over 600 coral species and 2,000 fish species

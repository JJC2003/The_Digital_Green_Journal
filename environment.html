The Causes of Global Warming

Uncovering the human and natural forces heating our planet — and why the stakes have never been higher.

2026 6 min read The Digital Green Journal

Global warming refers to the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature, primarily driven by human activities since the Industrial Revolution. It is not a future threat — it is happening right now, and its causes are well understood by scientists around the world.

The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that keeps Earth warm enough to support life. When sunlight reaches Earth's surface, some is absorbed and converted to heat. Greenhouse gases (GHGs) — such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and water vapor — trap some of this heat, preventing it from escaping into space.

However, human activities have dramatically increased concentrations of these gases, intensifying the greenhouse effect far beyond natural levels. This enhanced greenhouse effect is the primary driver of global warming.

Major Human Causes

1. Burning of Fossil Fuels

The combustion of coal, oil, and natural gas for electricity, transportation, and industrial processes is the single largest source of greenhouse gas emissions. When fossil fuels burn, stored carbon is released as CO₂. The global energy sector alone accounts for over 70% of total greenhouse gas emissions.

2. Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂ from the atmosphere. When forests are cleared — for agriculture, logging, or urban development — stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere. Deforestation contributes approximately 10–15% of annual greenhouse gas emissions globally. The Amazon, Congo Basin, and Southeast Asian rainforests are among the most threatened regions.

3. Agriculture and Livestock

Agricultural activities produce significant amounts of methane and nitrous oxide — greenhouse gases far more potent than CO₂. Methane is released by livestock during digestion (enteric fermentation) and from rice paddies and manure management. Nitrous oxide comes from synthetic fertilizers. Together, agriculture accounts for about 10–12% of global emissions.

4. Urbanization and Land Use Change

The expansion of cities and infrastructure transforms natural land into built environments that absorb more heat — the "urban heat island" effect — and produce large volumes of emissions from construction, waste, and transportation.

5. Industrial Processes and Waste

Cement production, steel manufacturing, and chemical processing release CO₂ and other pollutants. Landfills generate methane as organic waste decomposes without oxygen.

Key Greenhouse Gases and Their Sources

  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) — Fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, cement production
  • Methane (CH₄) — Livestock, rice farming, natural gas leaks, landfills
  • Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) — Synthetic fertilizers, livestock manure, combustion
  • Fluorinated Gases — Air conditioning, refrigeration, industrial manufacturing

Natural Contributing Factors

While human activities are the dominant driver, natural factors can also influence climate. Volcanic eruptions release CO₂ and aerosols; variations in solar activity can alter the energy reaching Earth; and natural ocean-atmosphere cycles like El Niño temporarily shift global temperatures.

However, scientists at NASA, NOAA, and the IPCC have consistently confirmed that natural factors alone cannot explain the speed and scale of warming observed since the 1950s. The fingerprint of human activity is unmistakable.

"It is unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land. Widespread and rapid changes in the climate system have occurred."
IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6), 2021

Why This Matters for the Philippines

The Philippines contributes less than 1% of global greenhouse gas emissions, yet it ranks among the world's most climate-vulnerable nations. Rising sea levels threaten coastal communities, intensifying typhoons endanger lives and livelihoods, and shifting monsoon patterns threaten food security.

The Science Behind the Heat

The Causes of Global Warming

Global warming is the long-term rise in Earth's average surface temperature caused primarily by human activities. Understanding its root causes is the first step toward meaningful action.

⏱ 5 min read

What is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the gradual increase in the overall temperature of Earth's atmosphere, primarily due to increased levels of CO₂, methane, and other greenhouse gases. These gases trap heat from the sun — a phenomenon known as the greenhouse effect.

Since the late 19th century, Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C — and continues to climb due to human industrial activity.

"We are the first generation to feel the effect of climate change and the last generation that can do something about it."

— Barack Obama, UN Climate Summit, 2014

Major Human Causes

🏭

Burning of Fossil Fuels

Coal, oil, and natural gas combustion accounts for over 75% of global GHG emissions, releasing massive CO₂ directly into the atmosphere.

🌳

Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks. Globally, 10 million hectares of forest are lost each year, releasing stored carbon.

🐄

Agriculture & Livestock

Cattle farming produces methane through digestion. Rice paddies and fertilizers release nitrous oxide (N₂O).

🏗️

Industrial Processes

Cement, iron, steel, and chemical industries contribute roughly 21% of global emissions.

🗑️

Waste & Landfills

Decomposing organic waste generates methane — 80 times more potent than CO₂ over 20 years.

❄️

Refrigerants & F-Gases

Fluorinated gases trap thousands of times more heat than CO₂, with enormous global warming potential.

⚠️

Philippines at Risk

Despite contributing less than 1% of global GHG emissions, the Philippines faces disproportionate impacts — stronger typhoons, rising sea levels, and prolonged droughts.

Natural vs. Human Contribution

Scientific consensus backed by NASA and IPCC confirms that human activities are responsible for over 95% of warming observed since the mid-20th century. The speed of current warming far exceeds any natural cycle.

Understanding Climate Change

Climate change refers to broad shifts in global and regional climate patterns including temperature, precipitation, wind patterns, and seasons — attributed largely to human-induced greenhouse gas emissions.

The IPCC states that evidence for human-caused climate change is now "unequivocal." Without urgent action, we are on track for a 2.5–4°C rise by end of century.

Global Impacts

🌊

Rising Sea Levels

Melting polar ice caps are causing sea levels to rise, threatening coastal cities. Manila Bay has been rising faster than the global average.

🔥

Extreme Heat Waves

More frequent heat waves are causing heat-related illnesses, stressing energy grids and endangering vulnerable populations.

🌧️

Erratic Rainfall

Some regions face prolonged droughts while others experience devastating floods, disrupting agriculture and water supply.

🐟

Ocean Acidification

As oceans absorb more CO₂, they become more acidic — threatening coral reefs and fishing communities in the Philippines.

Impact on the Philippines

The Philippines ranks among the top five countries most affected by climate change per the Global Climate Risk Index. With over 7,600 islands and a 36,000 km coastline, the country is extremely exposed to rising seas and intensifying typhoons.

Filipino farmers and fishermen face increasing vulnerability. Crop failures, saltwater intrusion, and reduced fish stocks directly threaten national food security.

"Climate change is no longer a distant threat — it is happening here, it is happening now, and the Filipino people are among its most affected."

— PAGASA, Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration
💡

The Role of Youth

Young Filipinos are at the forefront of climate advocacy — from community cleanups to lobbying for stronger climate policies across the country.

The Effect of Biodiversity Loss

Biodiversity is the foundation of healthy ecosystems and human wellbeing. Its rapid decline poses one of the gravest threats to life as we know it.

⏱ 6 min read

What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity encompasses the variety of life at all levels: species diversity, genetic diversity, and ecosystem diversity. We are currently losing species at a rate 1,000 times faster than the natural background — an event scientists call the Sixth Mass Extinction.

Major Drivers of Biodiversity Loss

🌾

Habitat Destruction

Conversion of forests and wetlands is the #1 driver. About 50% of Earth's habitable land is now used for agriculture.

🔥

Climate Change

Rising temperatures push species beyond tolerance limits, disrupting breeding, migration, and food availability.

☠️

Overexploitation

Overfishing, illegal wildlife trade, and unsustainable hunting collapse populations. The global wildlife trade is worth over $23 billion annually.

🦠

Invasive Species

Non-native species outcompete or prey on native species, disrupting ecological balance.

🧪

Pollution

Pesticides, heavy metals, and plastic waste contaminate soil, water, and air — poisoning species and degrading habitats.

🏙️

Urbanization

Urban sprawl fragments natural habitats. Cities now cover over 3% of Earth's land area, with growth accelerating.

Consequences

Collapse of Ecosystem Services

Pollination, water purification, flood control, and climate regulation all depend on healthy ecosystems.

Food Security Threats

Over 75% of global food crops depend on animal pollination. The decline of bees and butterflies directly threatens food production.

Loss of Medicinal Resources

25–50% of pharmaceutical drugs are derived from natural organisms. The Philippines' rich flora is a critical resource at risk.

Increased Disease Transmission

Reduced biodiversity allows disease vectors like mosquitoes and rodents to proliferate, increasing dengue and leptospirosis risks.

"We are eroding the very foundations of our economies, livelihoods, food security, health, and quality of life worldwide."

— IPBES Global Assessment Report, 2019
🦅

The Philippine Eagle: A Symbol at Risk

The Philippine Eagle is critically endangered, with fewer than 800 individuals remaining in the wild — mirroring the broader devastation of Philippine biodiversity.

⚖️ Republic Act 9003

The Ecological Solid Waste Management Act of 2000 mandates LGUs to establish MRFs, phase out open dumpsites, and implement source segregation. Enforcement remains a major challenge.

The Scale of the Problem

The Philippines generates over 40,000 tonnes of solid waste daily. Metro Manila alone accounts for about 9,000 tonnes per day. Open dumpsites, illegal dumping, and waterways clogged with garbage are common across the country.

Improper disposal leads to contaminated water sources, health hazards, flooding, and significant greenhouse gas emissions as organic waste decomposes anaerobically in open dumps.

The Philippine Legal Framework

The Philippines enacted Republic Act 9003 — the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act of 2000 — mandating closure of open dumpsites, source segregation into biodegradable, recyclable, and residual categories, and directing LGUs to set up Material Recovery Facilities (MRFs).

Implementation has been inconsistent. Many LGUs lack funds and technical capacity to comply. The National Solid Waste Management Commission (NSWMC) continues to push for reform.

Plastic Pollution & Ocean Impact

The Philippines ranked as the third largest contributor to ocean plastic pollution globally. Sachets — small single-use plastic packets — are particularly problematic because they cannot be recycled and easily enter waterways.

Pasig River in Metro Manila was once considered among the most polluted rivers in the world, carrying massive plastic volumes into Manila Bay and the ocean. Root-cause solutions including extended producer responsibility are urgently needed.

✅

Extended Producer Responsibility Act (2022)

Republic Act 11898 requires companies producing plastic packaging to develop EPR programs to recover and divert plastic waste — a major step toward corporate accountability.

Community-Level Solutions

🔄

Materials Recovery Facilities

MRFs sort, process, and recover recyclable and compostable materials before residual waste reaches landfills.

🌱

Composting Programs

Converting organic waste into compost reduces landfill burden and produces valuable soil amendment for community gardens.

🏫

School Waste Programs

School-based segregation and eco-brick initiatives build long-term environmental values in students.

Why Hierarchy Matters

The 5R's form a hierarchy of sustainability. Refusing and reducing prevent waste from being created — always more impactful than managing it afterward. Reusing extends product life. Repairing keeps materials in circulation. Recycling, while valuable, still consumes energy — making it the last line of defense.

True sustainability begins with conscious consumption.

"There is no such thing as 'away.' When we throw something away, it must go somewhere."

— Annie Leonard, The Story of Stuff

Everyday 5R Actions for Students

🍱

Bring Your Own (BYO)

Use a reusable lunchbox, tumbler, and utensils every day. Refuse disposable plastics at canteens.

📚

Reduce Paper Waste

Print on both sides, use digital notes, share files digitally. Choose e-books when possible.

👕

Extend Clothing Life

Repair torn clothes instead of discarding. Swap or donate garments. Avoid fast fashion impulse buys.

🔌

Reduce Energy Use

Turn off lights, unplug chargers. Reducing electricity demand lowers carbon emissions from power plants.

🗂️

Segregate & Recycle Properly

Sort waste into biodegradable, recyclable, and residual as required by RA 9003. Bring recyclables to your barangay MRF.

🌱

The Bigger Picture

If every Filipino practiced even two of the 5R's consistently, the country could divert millions of tonnes of waste from landfills and oceans annually.

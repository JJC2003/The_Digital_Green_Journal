The Science Behind the Heat

The Causes of Global Warming

Global warming is the long-term rise in Earth's average surface temperature caused primarily by human activities. Understanding its root causes is the first step toward meaningful action.

5 min read

What is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the gradual increase in the overall temperature of Earth's atmosphere, primarily due to the increased levels of carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and other greenhouse gases (GHGs). These gases trap heat from the sun, preventing it from escaping back into space — a phenomenon known as the greenhouse effect.

While the greenhouse effect is natural and necessary for life, human activities have drastically intensified it, causing temperatures to rise at an alarming rate. Since the late 19th century, the Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C — and continues to climb.

"We are the first generation to feel the effect of climate change and the last generation that can do something about it."

— Barack Obama, United Nations Climate Summit, 2014

Major Human Causes

🏭

Burning of Fossil Fuels

Coal, oil, and natural gas combustion for electricity, transportation, and manufacturing accounts for over 75% of global GHG emissions. This releases massive amounts of CO₂ directly into the atmosphere.

🌳

Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂. When trees are cut or burned — especially in tropical regions like the Philippines — they release stored carbon. Globally, 10 million hectares of forest are lost each year.

🐄

Agriculture & Livestock

Livestock farming — particularly cattle — produces methane through digestion. Rice paddies and the use of nitrogen fertilizers also release potent greenhouse gases like nitrous oxide (N₂O).

🏗️

Industrial Processes

Cement production, iron and steel manufacturing, and chemical industries emit large quantities of CO₂ and other GHGs. Industrial sources contribute roughly 21% of global emissions.

🗑️

Waste & Landfills

Decomposing organic waste in landfills generates methane, a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year period. Improper waste management worsens the problem significantly.

❄️

Refrigerants & F-Gases

Fluorinated gases (HFCs, PFCs, SF₆) used in refrigeration, air conditioning, and electronics can trap thousands of times more heat than CO₂. Though present in small quantities, they have an enormous global warming potential.

⚠️

Philippines at Risk

As a tropical archipelago, the Philippines is one of the world's most climate-vulnerable nations. Despite contributing less than 1% of global GHG emissions, it faces disproportionately severe impacts including stronger typhoons, rising sea levels, and prolonged droughts.

Natural vs. Human Contribution

It is important to note that natural factors — such as volcanic activity, solar cycles, and ocean circulation — do contribute to climate variability. However, scientific consensus, backed by organizations such as NASA and IPCC, confirms that human activities are responsible for over 95% of the warming observed since the mid-20th century. The speed of current warming far exceeds anything seen in natural cycles over thousands of years.

Understanding Climate Change

Climate change refers to broad shifts in global and regional climate patterns — including temperature, precipitation, wind patterns, and seasons — attributed largely to human-induced greenhouse gas emissions. It is not just about it getting "hotter"; it disrupts the entire climate system, creating unpredictable and extreme weather events worldwide.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) states that the evidence for human-caused climate change is now "unequivocal." Without urgent action, we are on track for a 2.5–4°C rise by the end of the century — with catastrophic consequences for ecosystems, food systems, and human civilization.

Global Impacts

🌊

Rising Sea Levels

Melting polar ice caps and glaciers are causing sea levels to rise, threatening low-lying coastal cities and island nations. Manila Bay has been rising faster than the global average.

🔥

Extreme Heat Waves

More frequent and intense heat waves are causing heat-related illnesses and deaths, stressing energy grids and endangering outdoor workers and elderly populations.

🌧️

Erratic Rainfall

Some regions experience prolonged droughts while others face devastating floods. This unpredictability disrupts agriculture, water supply, and human settlements.

🐟

Ocean Acidification

As oceans absorb more CO₂, they become more acidic — threatening marine ecosystems, coral reefs, and the fishing communities that depend on them, especially in the Philippines.

Impact on the Philippines

The Philippines ranks among the top five countries most affected by climate change, according to the Global Climate Risk Index. With over 7,600 islands and a coastline of more than 36,000 km, the country is extremely exposed to rising seas, storm surges, and intensifying typhoons.

Filipino farmers and fishermen — the backbone of food production — are increasingly vulnerable to climate disruptions. Crop failures, saltwater intrusion into farmland, and warmer ocean temperatures reducing fish stocks are all direct consequences threatening national food security and rural livelihoods.

"Climate change is no longer a distant threat — it is happening here, it is happening now, and the Filipino people are among its most affected."

— PAGASA, Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration

What Can Be Done?

Addressing climate change requires action on two fronts: mitigation (reducing emissions) and adaptation (adjusting to current and future climate impacts). The Philippines, through PAGASA, DENR, and various local government units, is investing in early warning systems, mangrove restoration, climate-resilient agriculture, and disaster risk reduction strategies.

💡

The Role of Youth

Young Filipinos are increasingly at the forefront of climate advocacy — from local community cleanups to lobbying for stronger climate policies. Student organizations like Kabataan para sa Kalikasan are driving awareness and action across the country.

Life's Web Unraveling

The Effect of Biodiversity Loss

Biodiversity — the variety of life on Earth — is the foundation of healthy ecosystems and human wellbeing. Its rapid decline poses one of the gravest threats to life as we know it.

6 min read

What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity encompasses the variety of life on Earth across all levels: species diversity (the number of different species), genetic diversity (variety within species), and ecosystem diversity (the range of habitats and ecological processes). Together, they form the web of life that supports all living things — including humans.

Scientists estimate there are between 8 and 10 million species on Earth. Yet we are currently losing species at a rate 1,000 times faster than the natural background extinction rate — an event many scientists now call the Sixth Mass Extinction.

Major Drivers of Biodiversity Loss

🌾

Habitat Destruction

Conversion of forests, wetlands, and grasslands into agricultural and urban land is the #1 driver of biodiversity loss. About 50% of Earth's habitable land is now used for agriculture.

🔥

Climate Change

Rising temperatures and shifting weather patterns push species beyond their tolerance limits, disrupting breeding, migration, and food availability.

☠️

Overexploitation

Overfishing, illegal wildlife trade, and unsustainable hunting drive populations of many species to collapse. The global wildlife trade is worth over $23 billion annually.

🦠

Invasive Species

Non-native species introduced to new environments outcompete or prey on native species, disrupting ecological balance and threatening endemics.

🧪

Pollution

Pesticides, heavy metals, plastic waste, and oil spills contaminate soil, water, and air — poisoning species and degrading critical habitats like coral reefs and wetlands.

🏙️

Urbanization

Urban sprawl fragments natural habitats, creating barriers to species movement and survival. Cities now cover over 3% of Earth's land area, with growth accelerating.

Consequences of Biodiversity Loss

The loss of biodiversity is not merely an ecological tragedy — it has profound and direct consequences for human society. Every species in an ecosystem plays a role, and when one disappears, it sets off a cascade of effects:

Collapse of Ecosystem Services

Pollination, water purification, soil fertility, flood control, and climate regulation all depend on healthy, diverse ecosystems. Biodiversity loss weakens these services that we rely on for survival.

Food Security Threats

Over 75% of global food crops depend on animal pollination. The global decline of bees, butterflies, and other pollinators directly threatens food production worldwide, including in the Philippines.

Loss of Medicinal Resources

About 25–50% of pharmaceutical drugs are derived from natural organisms. As species disappear, so do potential cures for diseases we haven't yet encountered. The Philippines' rich flora is a critical resource at risk.

Increased Disease Transmission

Biodiverse ecosystems act as buffers, regulating disease transmission. When biodiversity decreases, disease vectors like mosquitoes and rodents proliferate, increasing risks of outbreaks like dengue and leptospirosis.

"We are eroding the very foundations of our economies, livelihoods, food security, health, and quality of life worldwide."

— IPBES Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services, 2019
🦅

The Philippine Eagle: A Symbol at Risk

The Philippine Eagle — the national bird and one of the world's largest and most powerful eagles — is critically endangered, with fewer than 800 individuals remaining in the wild. Its decline mirrors the broader devastation of Philippine biodiversity driven by deforestation and habitat loss.

⚖️ Republic Act 9003

The Ecological Solid Waste Management Act of 2000 mandates LGUs to establish Materials Recovery Facilities (MRFs), phase out open dumpsites, and implement segregation at source. Enforcement remains a major challenge.

The Scale of the Problem

The Philippines generates over 40,000 tonnes of solid waste daily, and this figure continues to grow with urbanization and population growth. Metro Manila alone accounts for about 9,000 tonnes per day — yet its waste infrastructure is severely strained. Open dumpsites, illegal dumping, and waterways clogged with garbage are common sights across the country.

The problem goes beyond aesthetics. Improper waste disposal leads to contaminated water sources, health hazards, flooding, and significant greenhouse gas emissions as organic waste decomposes anaerobically in open dumps producing methane.

Philippine Legal Framework

The Philippines enacted Republic Act 9003, or the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act of 2000, which was considered a landmark law at the time. It mandated the closure of all open dumpsites, required source segregation of waste into biodegradable, recyclable, and residual categories, and directed Local Government Units (LGUs) to set up Material Recovery Facilities (MRFs).

However, implementation has been inconsistent. Many LGUs lack the funds and technical capacity to comply. Over 2,500 open and controlled dumpsites remain operational across the country, many in violation of the law. The National Solid Waste Management Commission (NSWMC) continues to push for reform and compliance.

Plastic Pollution & Ocean Impact

The Philippines ranked as the third largest contributor to ocean plastic pollution globally according to a 2015 Ocean Conservancy report. Sachets — small single-use plastic packets for condiments, shampoo, and other products — are particularly problematic because they cannot be recycled and easily find their way into waterways.

Pasig River in Metro Manila was once considered among the most polluted rivers in the world, carrying tremendous volumes of plastic waste into Manila Bay and ultimately the ocean. Large-scale cleanup efforts have improved conditions, but root-cause solutions — including extended producer responsibility and plastic reduction policies — are still urgently needed.

✅

Extended Producer Responsibility Act (2022)

Republic Act 11898 requires companies producing plastic packaging to develop and implement EPR programs to recover and divert plastic waste from the environment. This is a major step toward holding corporations accountable for the waste they generate.

Community-Level Solutions

Despite systemic challenges, many communities and local governments are pioneering innovative solutions. Eco-brick programs, zero-waste markets, composting programs, and upcycling enterprises are springing up across the country. Schools, barangays, and youth organizations play a critical role in building a culture of waste responsibility from the ground up.

🔄

Materials Recovery Facilities

MRFs are mandated in every barangay to sort, process, and recover recyclable and compostable materials before residual waste reaches landfills.

🌱

Composting Programs

Converting organic/biodegradable waste into compost reduces landfill burden and produces valuable soil amendment for urban and community gardens.

🏫

School Waste Programs

School-based segregation and eco-brick initiatives engage students in hands-on sustainability practices and build long-term environmental values.

Why Hierarchy Matters

The 5R's are not equal in environmental impact — they form a hierarchy of sustainability. Refusing and reducing prevent waste from being created in the first place, which is always more impactful than managing waste after the fact. Reusing extends product life. Repairing keeps materials in circulation. Recycling, while valuable, still consumes energy and resources — making it the last line of defense, not the first.

Many people default to recycling because it feels constructive — but without practicing the first four R's, recycling alone cannot address the scale of our waste crisis. True sustainability begins with conscious consumption.

"There is no such thing as 'away.' When we throw something away, it must go somewhere."

— Annie Leonard, The Story of Stuff

The 5R's in the Philippine Context

In the Philippines, the 5R framework is increasingly being integrated into school curricula, community programs, and national policy. The DENR and NSWMC regularly promote waste segregation and reduction campaigns. Local heroes like Zero Waste Negros and the barangay-level eco-stores in Quezon City demonstrate that the 5R lifestyle is achievable even in urban Filipino settings.

The emergence of zero-waste stores, package-free shopping, and community repair cafés across Metro Manila, Cebu, and Davao proves growing demand for sustainable alternatives. However, systemic support — including better MRF infrastructure, producer responsibility laws, and public education — is essential to scale these efforts nationally.

Everyday 5R Actions for Students

🍱

Bring Your Own (BYO)

Use a reusable lunchbox, tumbler, and utensils every day. Refuse disposable plastics at canteens and fast food outlets.

📚

Reduce Paper Waste

Print on both sides, use digital notes, and share files digitally. Choose e-books when possible.

👕

Extend Clothing Life

Repair torn clothes instead of discarding them. Swap or donate garments. Avoid fast fashion impulse buys.

🔌

Reduce Energy Use

Turn off lights, unplug chargers, and use devices efficiently. Reducing electricity demand lowers carbon emissions from power plants.

🗂️

Segregate & Recycle Properly

Sort waste into biodegradable, recyclable, and residual as required by RA 9003. Bring recyclables to your barangay's MRF.

🌱

The Bigger Picture

If every Filipino practiced even two of the 5R's consistently, the country could divert millions of tonnes of waste from landfills and oceans annually — drastically improving environmental quality and reducing the burden on overwhelmed waste management systems.

Our Final Thoughts

The Earth Needs Us.
Now More Than Ever.

The five topics explored in this blog — the causes of global warming, the far-reaching impacts of climate change, the devastating effects of biodiversity loss, the broken state of Philippine waste management, and the empowering framework of the 5R's — are not isolated issues. They are deeply interconnected threads of the same environmental crisis.

As Information Technology students, we have a unique power: the ability to communicate, amplify, and connect people to knowledge. This blog is more than an academic requirement — it is a call to action. Every article shared, every piece of information spread, and every conversation started contributes to the culture shift our planet so desperately needs.

The solutions exist. The science is clear. What is needed now is awareness, will, and collective action. It starts with each of us — in our homes, our schools, our barangays, and our communities. Refuse the unnecessary. Reduce what you consume. Reuse what you have. Repair before discarding. Recycle what remains.

Be the generation that turns the tide. 🌿
